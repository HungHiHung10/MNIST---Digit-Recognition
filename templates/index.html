Deep Learning Theory

How a CNN reads MNIST digits

AI
Antigravity Published Jul 4, 2026
3 min read

The model starts with a normalized 28x28 grayscale image, extracts local stroke patterns through convolution, compresses strong activations with pooling, then converts the learned representation into 10 digit probabilities.

1. Image Preprocessing

The uploaded or drawn digit is first centered and normalized. The bounding box of the strokes is calculated so the digit fits perfectly inside the canvas, converting it into a single-channel 28x28 tensor. This ensures that the model always looks at digits of consistent size and scale, regardless of how they were drawn.

2. Convolution & ReLU Activation

Small 3x3 kernels slide across the image, reacting strongly to local features like edges, curves, holes, and corners. In our architecture, the first layer learns 10 different kernels.

After convolution, the ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) activation function is applied. It keeps positive responses and completely blocks negative signals, effectively highlighting only the activated regions in the feature map.

"Convolution layers combine small strokes into larger, more meaningful patterns like the loop of a 9 or the corner of a 4."

3. Max Pooling

Pooling takes the strongest signal in each small 2x2 window. This not only reduces the map size—making the network much faster to train—but also makes the model robust to slight translations. If a stroke shifts by one pixel, the max pooling layer will still capture its strongest activation.

4. Dense & Softmax

Finally, the two-dimensional feature maps are flattened into a one-dimensional vector. The Dense layer learns to combine all the spatial information—curves, spaces, and intersections—into raw scores (logits) for each digit.

The Softmax function converts these logits into a neat probability distribution from 0 to 9. The longest bar on the output chart represents the digit the model is most confident in.
